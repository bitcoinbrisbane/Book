## ERC777

The ERC777 standard was suggested in late 2017 and, according to the [EIP abstract](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-777), claims to define:

> *This standard defines a new way to interact with a token contract while remaining backward compatible with [ERC-20](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-20).*
>
> *It defines advanced features to interact with tokens. Namely, **operators** to send tokens on behalf of another address — contract or regular account — and send/receive **hooks** to offer token holders more control over their tokens.*
>
> *It takes advantage of [ERC-1820](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-1820) to find out whether and where to notify contracts and regular addresses when they receive tokens, as well as to allow compatibility with already-deployed contracts.*

There are three ideas worth pulling out:

- **Operators** are addresses authorised to move tokens on behalf of a holder. They're a cleaner version of ERC20's `approve` + `transferFrom` allowance dance, with explicit authorisation and revocation.
- **Send / receive hooks** let a contract react when it sends or receives tokens. This is the big change — ERC20 sends are passive, ERC777 sends can trigger logic on either side.
- **ERC-1820** is a global registry that maps an address to the interfaces it implements. ERC777 uses it to discover whether the recipient of a transfer wants to be notified — without it, every contract on the chain would need to know about every token in advance.

ERC777 ended up with mixed adoption. The hook mechanism turned out to be a re-entrancy vector in poorly-written receivers (the imBTC incident in 2020 is the canonical example), so many production teams default back to ERC20 plus an explicit allowance. It's worth knowing about as architecture, less worth deploying without an audit.
