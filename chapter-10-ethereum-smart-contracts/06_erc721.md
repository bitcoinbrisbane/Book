## ERC721 — Non-Fungible Tokens

Arguably the second most-used contract standard on Ethereum — though most of the people interacting with it don't know it as a *contract standard*.

I'm talking, of course, about the infamous **NFTs**. The high-water mark for cultural moment was a **Bored Ape Yacht Club (BAYC)** NFT fetching **$3.4 million USD** at a Sotheby's auction in October 2021. ERC721 is the contract interface underneath all of that.

The [EIP-721 abstract](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-721) is succinct:

> *The following standard allows for the implementation of a standard API for NFTs within smart contracts. This standard provides basic functionality to track and transfer NFTs.*
>
> *We considered use cases of NFTs being owned and transacted by individuals as well as consignment to third party brokers/wallets/auctioneers ("operators"). NFTs can represent ownership over digital or physical assets. We considered a diverse universe of assets, and we know you will dream up many more:*
>
> - *Physical property — houses, unique artwork*
> - *Virtual collectibles — unique pictures of kittens, collectible cards*
> - *"Negative value" assets — loans, burdens and other responsibilities*
>
> *In general, all houses are distinct and no two kittens are alike. NFTs are **distinguishable** and you must track the ownership of each one separately.*

That last sentence is the operational difference from ERC20. Where ERC20 maps an address to a balance (one big `mapping(address => uint256)`), ERC721 maps a *token ID* to its owner (`mapping(uint256 => address)`) — every token is its own thing, and every transfer is per-token.

This is where the chapter on "anti-patterns" earlier becomes relevant. The standard's third use case — *"negative value" assets — loans, burdens and other responsibilities* — is a quiet acknowledgement that NFTs are an *ownership-of-something* primitive, not a JPEG primitive. The JPEG is typically stored off-chain (IPFS, sometimes worse). The chain tracks who owns the token. **What that token *means* is contract-specific, and frequently nothing.**

## Extensions worth knowing

ERC-721 is small on purpose. A flock of follow-up proposals layers richer behaviour on top of it:

- **[EIP-5643](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-5643) — Subscription NFTs.** Adds an `expiresAt` per token. Covered on the next page; it's what you reach for if you want a token to time-out.
- **[EIP-6997](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-6997) — Validated transfers and approvals.** Adds a *validation* step before `transferFrom`, `approve`, and `setApprovalForAll` actually execute. Each operation becomes a *pending* validation that a separate validator address has to confirm — the original goal is to defeat wallet-drain attacks, where a leaked key would otherwise let an attacker move every NFT out in a single transaction. (Still in peer review at the time of writing — worth tracking but not yet a finalised standard.)
- **[EIP-2981](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-2981) — Royalty payments.** Lets an NFT declare a royalty percentage and recipient. Honoured (or not) by marketplaces; OpenSea's history with this standard is its own saga.
- **[EIP-4906](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-4906) — Metadata update event.** A standard event for "this token's URI changed" so explorers and indexers stop having to poll.

These are non-binding interfaces. Implementing them means your token plays nicely with the tools that *also* implement them; not implementing them means you forfeit that interoperability. Same network-effect pattern as ERC20 in the previous chapter.
