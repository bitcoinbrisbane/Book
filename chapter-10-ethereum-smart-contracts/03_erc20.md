## The ERC20 standard

The **ERC20** (Ethereum Request for Comment 20) standard is the pivotal framework for creating and managing **fungible tokens** on Ethereum. It establishes a common set of rules and interfaces that smart contracts and decentralised applications can adhere to, ensuring compatibility and interoperability among the thousands of tokens that have been deployed on top of it. The introduction of ERC20 marked a significant milestone in the development of the Ethereum ecosystem — and played a crucial role in the explosion of the initial coin offering (ICO) phenomenon in 2017.

## A short history

ERC20 was first proposed by **Fabian Vogelsteller** and **Vitalik Buterin** in November 2015. Fabian, a blockchain developer (later well known for ERC725 and Lukso), and Vitalik, the co-founder of Ethereum, collaborated to design a standardised token interface that would simplify creating and deploying tokens on the Ethereum blockchain. This standardisation didn't just make it easier for developers to issue tokens — it enhanced the liquidity and exchangeability of those tokens, because every exchange and wallet only had to integrate the standard *once*.

The ERC20 standard was formalised and ratified by the Ethereum community in late 2015 and early 2016. Its acceptance quickly gained momentum as projects and startups recognised the advantages of using ERC20 tokens for crowdfunding and decentralised applications. That widespread adoption contributed to the rapid growth and diversification of the Ethereum ecosystem, and made the platform the dominant venue for token creation and smart contract development to this day.

## Why standards win

By introducing a standard interface, DApps gain a **network effect**:

- Wallets that speak ERC20 — MetaMask, Rainbow, Frame — display *every* ERC20 token without needing per-token code.
- Exchanges that integrate the standard can list any ERC20 by adding a row to a database.
- Indexers and explorers parse balance changes and transfers uniformly.
- Composability with other contracts (DEXs, lending, vaults) is automatic.

A token that doesn't implement ERC20 forfeits all of that. The standard isn't *legally* required, but practically it is: a non-standard token is invisible to the ecosystem.

## The shape of an ERC20 token

A token contract attempts mimics the model of company shares. There's a fixed (or controlled) supply, balances are tracked per holder, and transfers move ownership between addresses.

The ERC20 interface enforces a small set of read-only metadata fields:

- **`name`** — the human-readable name of the token (e.g. "USD Coin").
- **`symbol`** — the ticker. Convention is three or four characters (e.g. "USDC"), but it isn't enforced — Aave's token is "AAVE", Dai is "DAI", Uniswap is "UNI". Choose something memorable and check it isn't already in use on a major chain.
- **`totalSupply`** — the total number of tokens that exist. Returned as a `uint256`.
- **`decimals`** — how many decimal places the token uses for display. Most tokens use 18 (matching Ether), but stablecoins commonly use 6 (USDC) or 8 (WBTC). This field is *display only* — the EVM works in integer base units regardless.

These four fields tell wallets and explorers how to render your token. They don't move money on their own. The transfer functions, allowance machinery, and events are next.
